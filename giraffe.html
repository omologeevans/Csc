Giraffe

Giraffes are the tallest land animals, reaching heights of up to 18 feet. They possess long necks and distinctive spotted coats, which help them blend into their native African savannas.

Their prehensile tongues allow them to grasp and eat leaves from tall trees. Despite their imposing appearance, giraffes are gentle herbivores that typically form loose social groups.

Physical Features:

  • Giraffes are instantly recognizable by their towering height and long neck.

  • Their coat features irregularly shaped patches, unique to each individual, and provides camouflage against trees.

  • Ossicones, horn-like structures, adorn their heads, and they have a long, prehensile tongue.

Special Abilities:

  • A prehensile tongue, up to 45 centimeters long, allows giraffes to reach leaves high in trees, which many other herbivores cannot access.

  • Giraffes have a strong field of vision, an essential advantage for spotting predators from a distance.
